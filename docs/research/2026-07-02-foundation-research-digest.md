## Prior art — platform-independent ownership attempts

Two decades of platform-independent ownership attempts split into two families, and both fail for reasons unrelated to their core technology. The consortium family (UltraViolet, 2011-2019) proved a neutral rights registry can work architecturally — Neustar's Coordinator held only entitlement records, so shutdown was survivable via retailer migration — but died because the most powerful rights holder (Disney) refused and built a rival, and the dominant retailers (Apple, Amazon, Google) never integrated. Its winner, Movies Anywhere (14.5M users, five studios, ~10,000 films in 2026), succeeds precisely because it is not neutral: Disney owns it and can compel participation — yet Google's month-long 2025 walkout shows even that interop is a revocable business courtesy. The blockchain family (Robot Cache, GameStop NFT, Ubisoft Quartz, Royal, Sound.xyz, OneOf) added token persistence but never decentralized the fulfillment obligation: Robot Cache's downloaded games bricked at its May 2026 shutdown despite blockchain branding; Ubisoft's Digits persist uselessly on Tezos; Royal hit the Howey wall. Recurring killers, in rough order of lethality: rights-holder non-participation; off-chain fulfillment dying with the company; speculator-only demand (Robot Cache booked $532 revenue in 2024); securities exposure whenever ownership includes profit share; crypto stigma; and consortium governance latency. Survivors (Book.io, Anotherblock, Catalog) are tiny, consumptive-framed, or pushed compliance to specialists; the 2025 wave (Story Protocol) pivoted to B2B programmable IP licensing for AI. Meanwhile regulation (California AB 2426) and consumer politics (Stop Killing Games, 1.45M signatures) are creating genuine tailwinds for verifiable-license infrastructure — the demand is real; every failure was on the supply and fulfillment side.

### Design implications

- Separate the license registry from content delivery and DRM (UltraViolet's Coordinator model): the registry should store portable entitlement records that multiple independent fulfillers can honor, so the platform's death does not destroy access.
- Design end-of-life into the protocol from day one: contractual/technical graceful degradation (key escrow, DRM-free fallback copies, or automatic entitlement migration to other retailers) is the single feature that separates UltraViolet's wind-down from Robot Cache's game-bricking — and it is becoming a regulatory expectation (Stop Killing Games, AB 2426).
- Treat rights-holder acquisition as the core product problem, not adoption of the tech: even a 70%-of-resale revenue share (Robot Cache) failed to attract catalogs. Without an anchor tenant with market power or a regulatory mandate, neutral registries stall; plan for indie/backlist/public-domain seeding rather than fighting incumbents for AAA/frontlist content.
- Get the retailers/clients, not just the rights holders: UltraViolet had nearly every studio but died because Apple, Amazon and Google refused to integrate. An open registry needs at least one mass-market consumption surface that honors its licenses natively.
- Never attach profit expectation to the license token: any fractional-royalty or resale-yield framing triggers Howey/securities analysis (Royal.io's death, GameStop's cited 'regulatory uncertainty'). Keep tokens strictly consumptive (proof-of-license), and route any revenue-share features through regulated specialist partners (the Anotherblock→Polytrade pattern).
- Hide the chain: every venture that led with NFT/crypto branding suffered stigma and speculator capture; survivors made blockchain invisible infrastructure (Sound Premium). Wallets and keys must be invisible by default — custodial onboarding with a guaranteed export-to-self-custody path — because mandatory self-custody is a UX killer and pure custody is fake ownership (Robot Cache).
- Token persistence ≠ license persistence: an on-chain record is only as valuable as the off-chain obligation it points to. Make the registry entry legally bind the rights holder (a real license grant with survivability terms), not just cryptographically bind a pointer to a service that can vanish.
- Store or escrow what's needed to exercise the license independently: Book.io's encrypted-asset approach (token gates decryption of content on decentralized storage) is the most credible survivability architecture observed, but it remains unproven at scale (~130k units, vendor-reported).
- Expect the interop layer to be attacked by bilateral business disputes: even Movies Anywhere lost Google for a month in 2025 over an unrelated carriage fight. Protocol design should minimize per-retailer bilateral dependencies (open verification APIs, no per-partner rights silos).
- Consider B2B/compliance positioning before consumer marketplace positioning: the only well-capitalized 2025-2026 entrant (Story Protocol) sells programmable IP licensing rails to businesses and AI companies, and AB 2426-style disclosure laws create a compliance market for verifiable license registries — a more defensible wedge than consumer resale, which structurally threatens rights holders' primary sales.
- Do not mistake vocal demand for purchase behavior: 1.45M petition signatures and Kindle backlash coexist with $532/year revenue at the most ownership-forward game store. Validate willingness-to-pay for ownership guarantees (resale value, permanence) before building marketplace liquidity features.
- Keep governance lightweight: DECE's ~90-member consortium took 2.5 years to ship a spec while Disney shipped a product. An open registry needs a small core spec team with credible neutrality, not committee-of-the-whole governance.

## Legal landscape — ownership, exhaustion, liability

The mid-2026 landscape splits cleanly. Exhaustion/resale: a statutory digital resale right exists only for software in the EU (UsedSoft, under strict conditions — perpetual license, full-value fee, seller's copy destroyed, no license splitting). Tom Kabinet forecloses exhaustion for all other digital works in the EU, and ReDigi forecloses it entirely in the US, so any resale/transfer feature must be a contractual, rightsholder-authorized license term recorded in a registry — never a legal default. Consumer-labeling law is the fastest-moving front: California AB 2426 (effective 2025-01-01) and Maryland HB 208 (2025-10-01) ban "buy" language for revocable licenses absent disclosures, class actions (Weber v. GameStop, Jan 2026) already attack entire purchase flows, and New York is pending. The Stop Destroying Videogames ECI (1,294,188 verified signatures) was answered on 2026-06-16: no legislation, only a voluntary industry code of conduct by end-2026 and a Digital Content Directive report; organizers pivoted to the Digital Fairness Act (proposal expected Q3 2026). Liability: DSA arts 4-8 and DMCA §512 shield neutral conduits/hosts that run notice-and-takedown, but CDSM art 17 imposes direct liability on for-profit content-sharing platforms, and Grokster, Ziggo and YouTube/Cyando make purpose and knowing facilitation the decisive tests — no architecture routes around inducement, and even a hosting-nothing index can infringe. Encrypted unauthorized copies remain copies (17 U.S.C. §101 machine-aided perceptibility; InfoSoc art 2 "any manner or form"), though sharded-storage liability is litigation-untested. Anti-circumvention law (§1201, upheld in Green v. DOJ; InfoSoc art 6) bars DRM-stripping even of one's own purchases; no format-shifting exemption exists in either system.

### Design implications

- Separate the registry from the content: an open ledger of licenses/entitlements (metadata, attestations, transfer records) carries near-zero copyright risk; the moment the platform stores, indexes, or gateways the works themselves, the full §512/DSA/art-17 liability stack applies. Keep the registry layer content-free by design.
- Resale/transfer must be a rights-holder-granted contractual feature encoded in the license terms the registry records — not a platform default. Only exception: EU software distributed under perpetual, fully-paid licenses can support UsedSoft-compliant transfers (with proof the seller's copy is disabled and no license splitting); ebooks, music, films and game content cannot rely on exhaustion anywhere.
- Encryption does not launder infringement: encrypted or sharded unauthorized copies are still copies under US fixation doctrine and the EU reproduction right. The network must gate what enters distribution (only rightsholder-authorized payloads, verified at publish time), not rely on nodes being unable to read what they host.
- Every operated choke point (gateway, pinning service, index, search, default node software distribution) needs safe-harbor plumbing: US designated agent, repeat-infringer policy, notice-and-takedown; EU expeditious removal on knowledge (DSA art 6) plus a denylist mechanism nodes actually consume (IPFS badbits shows both the pattern and its limits — takedown never purges the network, and volunteer gateway operators become the enforcement targets).
- Avoid the OCSSP profile under CDSM art 17: if the platform's main purpose is giving public access to large amounts of user-uploaded protected content organized and promoted for profit, it is directly liable in the EU unless it licenses and filters. A license-registry with rightsholder-onboarded catalogs (authorization first, distribution second) inverts that risk; note the <3 years / <€10M startup carve-out is temporary.
- Inducement is the hard red line no technical design routes around (Grokster in the US; Ziggo/YouTube-Cyando in the EU): marketing, onboarding copy, defaults, and community moderation must never target or celebrate unauthorized use; indexing/curating infringing content creates liability even with zero hosting. Governance documents and public communications are legal-risk surfaces equal to code.
- No DRM-stripping or circumvention features, ever — not even 'import your own purchases': §1201 (constitutionality confirmed by Green v. DOJ, 2024) and InfoSoc art 6 prohibit circumvention and trafficking in tools regardless of fair use or private-copy exceptions, and the 2024 triennial granted no personal format-shifting exemption. Ingestion of user libraries must be limited to DRM-free files or publisher-blessed migration APIs.
- Label honestly or make it truly irrevocable: if an entitlement can be revoked, AB 2426/Maryland HB 208 forbid 'buy' language without acknowledgment/disclosure, and Weber v. GameStop shows courts examine the entire purchase funnel. The exemption for goods the seller cannot revoke (permanent offline download) is a design opportunity — a registry that makes entitlements technically irrevocable both escapes disclosure liability and matches the regulatory direction.
- Build end-of-life persistence into license templates now: the Commission's SKG response creates a voluntary code-of-conduct process by end-2026 and the Digital Fairness Act (Q3 2026 proposal) may harden it. Escrowed offline patches, server-sunset commitments, and license survival clauses are cheap to standardize in a registry schema and likely to become compliance assets.
- Use the DMA rather than fear it: a small decentralized platform is nowhere near gatekeeper thresholds, while Apple's EU obligations (alternative marketplaces, web distribution, anti-steering remedies, 5% CTC from 2026) open iOS as a distribution channel for an independent content store in the EU.
- Design for identifiable accountability at the edges: fully anonymous node operators cannot claim §512 safe harbors (agent registration, policies) and attract carpet-bomb DMCA campaigns; a two-tier network — accountable operated infrastructure with compliance machinery, plus thin verification-only peers that never relay content — tracks the legal grain.
- Jurisdiction-partition the feature set: EU-only UsedSoft software transfers, US/EU-wide contractual transfers where licensed, disclosure flows keyed to buyer state (CA/MD now, NY likely) — the registry schema should carry per-jurisdiction capability flags rather than assuming one global rule.

## Tech stack 2026 — storage, chains, wallets, encryption

The 2026 stack for a decentralized license registry plus content network is viable, but only with a layered architecture. Storage economics split cleanly by role: Filecoin Onchain Cloud warm storage (~$2.50/TiB/mo per copy, 2-copy minimum, PDP-verified, launched Jan 2026) and Storj ($7/TB/mo + $7/TB egress, $50/mo minimum from July 2026) handle petabyte media at $5-10k/PB/month, while Arweave (~$6.35-8/GB one-time) is only sane for small immutable license metadata, never video masters. 4K streaming works today, but exclusively through gateway/CDN layers (Storj's benchmarked parallel downloads, Pinata dedicated gateways with HLS at $20-100/mo tiers, FilCDN over Filecoin) — never raw IPFS gateways. Registry writes are effectively free everywhere: Base <$0.01, Arbitrum ~$0.004 (post-Dia), Polygon ~$0.002, Solana ~$0.0005; a $3,000+/month Conduit-style appchain only makes sense for sovereignty, not cost. Wallet UX is solved: 62M+ active smart accounts, EIP-7702 live since Pectra (May 2025), passkey signers via secp256r1, and Stripe-owned Privy / Coinbase CDP embedded wallets mean consumers can hold NFT licenses without seed phrases. Token-gated decryption is production-scale but churn-prone — Lit cycled Datil→Naga→v3 in six months (Naga sunset April 1, 2026) and TACo faces a H2 2026 fork — so abstract the key layer. Privacy: stealth addresses (ERC-5564, Umbra 77k+ addresses) deploy today; ZK ownership proofs are buildable; Aztec remains alpha. No license standard won: ERC-5218 is still the reference, Story Protocol pivoted away to AI data, and ERC-2981 royalties are unenforceable — design your own enforcement.

### Design implications

- Tier storage by role: license records and legal-text hashes on-chain + Arweave (one-time ~$7/GB is trivial for KB-scale metadata); video masters on Filecoin warm storage (~$5/TiB/mo for 2 copies) or Storj ($7/TB/mo); hot delivery through a CDN/gateway layer — budget roughly $5-10k/month per PB stored plus $7/TB egress-equivalent for delivery.
- Treat 4K streaming as a CDN problem, not a storage problem: serve HLS/DASH renditions through Storj S3 endpoints, Pinata dedicated gateways, or FilCDN; never point players at public IPFS gateways; content-address (CID) everything so the delivery layer stays swappable.
- Put the registry on a general-purpose L2 (Base or Arbitrum: sub-cent writes, EVM tooling, 4337/7702 support) or Solana if throughput dominates; defer any purpose-built appchain (>$3k/month RaaS baseline + ops burden) until write volume or governance sovereignty actually demands it.
- Design wallet onboarding around embedded wallets + passkeys from day one: ERC-4337 smart accounts with WebAuthn/secp256r1 signers, gas sponsorship via paymaster so buyers never hold ETH, social/passkey recovery instead of seed phrases; pick an embedded-wallet vendor (Privy/Stripe, Coinbase CDP) but keep key-export/portability as a hard requirement to avoid lock-in.
- For encrypted-at-rest content, use threshold access control (Lit v3 or TACo) but wrap it behind your own DEK-envelope abstraction: per-content data keys wrapped by the threshold network, so you can rotate providers (Lit's three network generations in six months and TACo's pending 2026 fork prove churn is the norm) without re-encrypting the media corpus.
- Do not rely on decryption gating alone for enforcement: once decrypted, content can be copied; combine threshold gating with watermarking/forensic marking and legal license terms, and treat the crypto layer as access control, not DRM.
- Bake purchase privacy in at the protocol level: support ERC-5564 stealth addresses for license receipt so buyers' collections are unlinkable, and design the license-verification API to accept a ZK proof of ownership (not a raw wallet address) so verifiers never learn the buyer's full on-chain history; keep fully-private Aztec-style deployment as a later phase, it is still alpha.
- Model the license as ERC-721 (exclusive/unique grants) or ERC-1155 (multi-edition), implementing an ERC-5218-style license tree (root license, sublicenses, revocation state) with dual machine-readable + hashed legal text, PIL-style; there is no dominant finalized license ERC in 2026, so publish your interface as an open spec to become a Schelling point.
- Do not build revenue assumptions on ERC-2981 marketplace royalty enforcement — it is declarative and widely bypassed; enforce economics in your own transfer logic, renewal/subscription state (ERC-6551 token-bound accounts can hold per-license state), or primary-sale margins.
- Learn from Story Protocol's pivot to AI-data provenance: general-purpose on-chain IP licensing hasn't found product-market fit as a standalone chain, but AI-training-data licensing is the demand wedge in 2026 — consider machine-readable license terms (usable-for-training flags, provenance attestations) as a first-class registry feature.
- Denominate storage and license payments in stablecoins (USDFC on Filecoin, USDC on L2s) — the 2026 stack (Filecoin Pay, paymasters accepting stablecoin gas) supports this end-to-end, insulating both creators and the availability network from token volatility.

## Market — wedge analysis

The 2024-2026 evidence is unusually one-directional: digital "ownership" failures are accelerating (Discovery near-wipe 2023, Funimation entitlement loss 2024, The Crew shutdown and lawsuits, Concord refunds, Kindle export removal Feb 2025, PS3/Vita store closures announced July 1, 2026, Robot Cache destroying all purchases May 2026), regulators have responded with disclosure duties rather than ownership rights (California AB 2426 effective Jan 2025; EU Commission on June 16, 2026 declined legislation for Stop Killing Games' 1.29M signatures but ordered an industry end-of-life code of conduct by end 2026), and the user-cited Sony claim is verified real: PlayStation disc production for new games ends January 2028, announced July 1, 2026. Meanwhile the DRM-free economy is healthy at the indie/mid tier: GOG (newly independent under co-founder Kiciński, $25M buyout, Dec 2025) runs a de facto proto-registry via its Preservation Program (267 games, delisting-proof commitments); itch.io hosts 200k DRM-free games; Bandcamp under Songtradr still pays out ($19M via Bandcamp Fridays in 2025); Tor has sold DRM-free ebooks since 2012. Video is closed off — interop belongs to studio consortia (Movies Anywhere, 14.5M users; UltraViolet's 30M-user corpse as warning). The credible v1 wedge is PC games with DRM-free-native storefronts — GOG as anchor partner, itch.io second, plus indie/mid-size publishers for whom a portable license record costs nothing. Every failed predecessor (UltraViolet, Robot Cache) died because entitlements lived inside one company; the registry's core design mandate is to be the thing that survives its members.

### Design implications

- Wedge choice: v1 = PC games vertical, anchored on GOG (newly independent, founder-owned, mission-aligned, thin margins force differentiation-by-trust, already operates delisting-proof preservation commitments and markets 'installers that cannot be taken away') with itch.io as second partner (200k DRM-free games, creator-set economics). Both need a standards story they cannot credibly build alone after Robot Cache proved single-company registries die with the company. Console and AAA are closed (Blizzard delisted Warcraft from GOG's program); video is locked inside the Movies Anywhere consortium; music/books are follow-on verticals, not v1.
- Survivability is the product: every prior system failed by coupling entitlement verification to one company's servers (Robot Cache 2026: installed games bricked; UltraViolet 2019: consortium dissolved). License records must be user-exportable, cryptographically signed, verifiable offline, and replicated across independent registry nodes — the registry must outlive any storefront, including its founders.
- A license without an artifact is worthless: pair every registry entry with a guaranteed re-download right for a DRM-free artifact (GOG offline-installer model), ideally with content hashes and mirror obligations across member stores. This is what distinguishes the registry from Steam's 'license on Steam' disclaimer.
- Regulatory tailwind timing: AB 2426 makes honest ownership language a compliance problem for stores; the EU Commission explicitly requested a voluntary industry code of conduct on game end-of-life by end 2026. Position the registry as the reference technical implementation of that code — engage the Commission process and consumer groups (UFC-Que Choisir, SKG's Digital Fairness Act push) in 2026 while the window is open.
- Publisher economics must be zero-marginal-cost: GOG got publishers to sign preservation commitments because it costs them nothing (funded from store fees). Fund the registry from storefront/platform membership, never per-title publisher fees; for indie publishers already shipping DRM-free on GOG/itch/Humble, joining should be a metadata flag, not an integration project.
- Governance must be neutral and censorship-resistant by design: itch.io's July 2025 payment-processor capitulation shows independent platforms get coerced through payment rails. Keep the registry content-neutral (it records licenses, it does not host or sell), so it cannot become — or be captured as — a chokepoint.
- Do not use blockchain framing: the demand signal MIDiA saw in blockchain gaming is real, but every implementation (Robot Cache, Ubisoft Quartz, GameStop NFT) failed and burned consumer trust. Ship boring, auditable, signed-receipt infrastructure; the pitch is 'your receipt outlives the store', not tokens or resale (resale triggers publisher resistance — defer it).
- Ride the news cycle: Sony's January 2028 disc cutoff (announced 2026-07-01) plus PS3/Vita store closures give an ~18-month narrative arc where 'physical is ending and digital is only a license' is mainstream news. Launch messaging and partner announcements should sync to these dates and to Bandcamp Friday / GOG Preservation waves for earned media.
- Design the demand-side loop: GOG Dreamlist (13M votes) and SKG (1.29M signatures) prove organized consumer energy exists; the registry should expose cross-store demand signals (which lost/delisted titles users hold licenses for) as a service to publishers — turning preservation demand into re-release revenue, mirroring GOG's business case.
- Books and music as v2, with named beachheads: music is already DRM-free (Bandcamp/labels — lowest friction, lowest urgency); books have fresh pain (Kindle export removal Feb 2025) and a proven DRM-free mid-size publisher precedent (Tor since 2012) but Big-5/Amazon lock-in blocks scale. Sequence: games → music (Bandcamp-style labels) → DRM-free book publishers; treat Hollywood video as out of scope until the registry has leverage.

## Preservation — legal landscape

The 2024-2026 record draws a sharp legal line: preservation copying by institutions is increasingly protected, while public remote access to preserved works is consistently refused. The VGHF/SPN study (July 2023, still the reference figure) found 87% of pre-2010 US games out of print, yet the October 2024 DMCA rulemaking denied the remote-access exemption after ESA lobbying, leaving only premises-limited library/archive/museum preservation exemptions — temporary ones, up for renewal in the tenth triennial cycle opened June 2026. Hachette v. Internet Archive ended December 2024 with controlled digital lending rejected as fair use, and the Great 78 suit settled confidentially in September 2025: single-entity archives asserting fair use for access have lost or retreated. On the mandate side, France's BnF is the standout precedent — legal deposit has covered video games since 1992 (22,000+ titles, emulation, accredited-researcher access) — while the UK regulations exclude games and the US has neither a mandate (online-only works deposit on demand only) nor room to create one easily after the Valancourt takings ruling. The EU is the most promising jurisdiction: CDSM Article 6 gives cultural heritage institutions a mandatory, contract-proof preservation exception that explicitly allows cross-border third parties to make copies on their behalf, and the Stop Destroying Videogames ECI (1.29M verified signatures) — though rebuffed by the Commission in June 2026 — produced a committed end-of-life industry code of conduct by end-2026 and an ongoing Digital Fairness Act amendment push. Bottom line: a decentralized archive network can operate legally today only as a federation of qualifying institutions (EFGAMP-style), anchored on deposit mandates where they exist and CDSM Article 6 technical-agent status in the EU, with access tiered per jurisdiction and no public remote play.

### Design implications

- Anchor nodes in qualifying institutions, not peers: every legal shelter found (US 1201 exemptions, CDSM Art. 6, deposit laws) attaches to libraries/archives/museums holding lawfully acquired copies — the platform should register institutional custodians and their qualification status, not anonymous hosts.
- Use CDSM Article 6 as the EU operating hook: the directive explicitly lets cultural heritage institutions rely on third parties, including cross-border ones, to make preservation copies, and overriding contract clauses are unenforceable — a decentralized network can legally act as the shared technical infrastructure of EU institutions if roles ('acting on behalf and under responsibility of institution X') are recorded in the registry.
- Treat access rights as per-jurisdiction, tiered metadata: preservation ≠ access everywhere (BnF: accredited researchers on-site; US: premises-only). The license registry must model who may access each item, where, and under which legal basis, with remote/public access off by default.
- Make commercial-availability status a first-class registry field: the 87% out-of-print metric and the 'online arcade' market-harm objection both turn on whether a work is in commerce — automated availability tracking creates the evidentiary trail any future exemption or code-of-conduct access will require.
- Version legal capabilities with expiry dates: US Section 1201 exemptions lapse every three years (next window Oct 2027–Oct 2030) — legal-basis records in the registry must be time-bound and renewable, never hardcoded as permanent.
- Record provenance/chain of custody per copy: 'permanently in the collection' (ownership, license, legal deposit, permanent custody) is the eligibility test under both EU and US exceptions — the registry should prove each node's source copy is lawful.
- Build an end-of-life deposit/escrow workflow: the EU Commission's industry code of conduct on game 'end of life' (due end-2026) and possible Digital Fairness Act amendments create a plausible hand-off channel for binaries, server components and keys at sunset — the platform should be ready to receive voluntary publisher deposits with machine-readable license terms, since no mandate covers digital-only/streaming content anywhere.
- Do not rely on fair use for access features: Hachette killed one-to-one CDL for in-commerce works and the Great 78 settlement shows even obsolete-format streaming attracts nine-figure claims — access beyond preservation must come from licenses, deposit statutes, or granted exemptions recorded in the registry.
- Decentralization mitigates the single-entity failure mode: Internet Archive's litigation and Embracer's corporate-dependent archive show concentration risk; replicate holdings across multiple institutional nodes in different jurisdictions (EFGAMP is the existing federation precedent), while keeping per-node legal compliance independent.
- Prioritize non-game contextual assets for open distribution: VGHF's Digital Library (magazines, press kits, dev documents — freely accessible since Jan 2025) shows a large corpus can be shared publicly with low legal risk; segment the registry so metadata and contextual materials flow openly while game binaries stay in the restricted tier.
- Don't design around hoped-for new mandates in the US: Valancourt makes uncompensated deposit expansion constitutionally fraught and the Copyright Office defers to rightsholders — for the US, plan on copyright-deposit copies, donations, and museum partnerships (Strong/University of Rochester model) rather than statutory deposit.
- France is the reference implementation for a deposit-mandate node: BnF's 1992-onward game deposit (including dematerialized formats and web archiving, emulation-based preservation, researcher-only access) is the proven ceiling of what a mandate currently delivers — model the platform's 'mandate-backed node' profile on it.

## Adversarial stress-test

### Kill factors

- **Gli incumbent (Sony, Apple, Amazon, Steam) non hanno alcun incentivo ad aderire — il problema dell'anchor tenant** — La tesi presume che le piattaforme mantengano il loro ~30% 'su infrastruttura condivisa', ma il 30% non è il loro asset: l'asset è il lock-in e la proprietà della relazione col cliente, che un registro aperto distrugge per definizione. Il precedente è esatto e devastante: UltraViolet aveva quasi tutti gli studios e 30M utenti ed è morto perché Apple, Amazon e Google si rifiutarono di integrare; Movies Anywhere funziona solo perché Disney può COSTRINGERE la partecipazione — e persino lì Google è uscita per un mese nel 2025 per una disputa commerciale non correlata. Robot Cache offriva ai publisher il 70% del revenue di resale e ha fatturato $532 nel 2024. 'Diventare così buoni da essere inevitabili' è esattamente la strategia con cui UltraViolet è morto: la qualità tecnica non ha mai vinto il rifiuto di integrazione dei retailer dominanti.
  - *Mitigation*: Rinunciare agli incumbent come target v1. Wedge sugli storefront DRM-free-native già mission-aligned per cui aderire costa zero: GOG (appena indipendente, $25M buyout, già gestisce un proto-registry col Preservation Program) e itch.io (200k giochi DRM-free). L'unica forcing function credibile verso i grandi è regolatoria (codice di condotta EU end-of-life entro fine 2026, Digital Fairness Act Q3 2026), non la superiorità del prodotto.
- **L'archivio 'tutto il catalogo globale' è infringement di massa così com'è concepito** — Nodi che ospitano tutti i film e giochi senza autorizzazione = riproduzione non autorizzata, e la crittografia/sharding non lava nulla: copie criptate restano copie (17 U.S.C. §101, InfoSoc art 2). L'accesso remoto pubblico a opere preservate è stato negato ovunque nel 2024-2026: esenzione DMCA remote-access respinta a ottobre 2024 dopo lobbying ESA, Hachette v. Internet Archive ha ucciso il controlled digital lending, Great 78 chiuso in settlement dopo pretese a nove cifre. Peggio: Grokster/Ziggo/YouTube-Cyando rendono l'inducement una linea rossa che NESSUNA architettura decentralizzata aggira, e il marketing stesso ('ospitiamo tutto ciò che le piattaforme cancellano') è evidenza di inducement. I nodi anonimi non hanno safe harbor §512 e diventano bersagli di campagne DMCA a tappeto (pattern IPFS badbits).
  - *Mitigation*: Ripartire l'archivio come federazione di istituzioni qualificate: CDSM art 6 permette esplicitamente a terzi cross-border di fare copie di preservazione per conto di biblioteche/archivi/musei EU (modello BnF/EFGAMP), con accesso tiered per giurisdizione e ZERO remote play pubblico; deposito volontario end-of-life dai publisher via il codice di condotta EU; materiali contestuali (riviste, press kit, dev doc, stile VGHF) nel tier aperto. È un progetto valido ma NON è 'tutto per tutti per sempre' — quella versione non ha alcun percorso legale.
- **L'attestazione on-chain non è la licenza: il gap di fulfillment rende la promessa centrale ineseguibile per il contenuto DRM'd** — Token persistence ≠ license persistence: Robot Cache ha brickato i giochi installati allo shutdown di maggio 2026 nonostante il branding blockchain; i Digits Ubisoft persistono inutilmente su Tezos. 'Consumare con qualsiasi client per sempre' richiede o artefatti DRM-free garantiti o rimozione del DRM — e §1201 (costituzionalità confermata in Green v. DOJ) e InfoSoc art 6 vietano la circumvention anche sui propri acquisti, senza alcuna esenzione di format-shifting nel 2024 triennial. Quindi per tutto il frontline Sony/Apple/Steam la promessa 'forever, any client' è legalmente impossibile senza consenso del rights holder — che per il kill-factor 1 non arriverà.
  - *Mitigation*: Vincolare ogni entry del registro a due obblighi concreti: (a) un grant di licenza legalmente vincolante con clausole di survivability (non un puntatore crittografico a un servizio che può sparire), e (b) diritto garantito di re-download di un artefatto DRM-free con content hash e obblighi di mirror tra store membri (modello installer GOG; Book.io per encrypted-asset è l'unica architettura di survivability credibile osservata, ma non provata a scala). Questo restringe il perimetro reale ai verticali DRM-free: accettarlo esplicitamente.
- **La domanda di ownership è vocale, non monetaria** — 1,29M firme verificate SKG e 13M voti su GOG Dreamlist coesistono con $532/anno di revenue allo store più ownership-forward mai esistito (Robot Cache 2024). Movies Anywhere ha 14,5M utenti perché è gratis e invisibile, non perché qualcuno paghi per la portabilità. I consumatori dichiarano di volere ownership ma comprano convenience. In più c'è il cold start bilaterale: senza cataloghi niente compratori, senza compratori nessun motivo per i cataloghi di integrarsi.
  - *Mitigation*: Mai far pagare il consumatore per la garanzia di ownership e mai far pagare il publisher per-titolo: onboarding publisher = flag di metadata a costo marginale zero (il modello con cui GOG ha ottenuto le firme sul Preservation Program), funding da membership degli storefront. Validare la willingness-to-pay con esperimenti a impegno economico reale PRIMA di costruire qualunque feature di marketplace o liquidità.
- **Branding crypto/NFT: stigma consumer + trappola securities (Howey)** — Ogni tentativo NFT-branded del settore è fallito bruciando trust: Robot Cache, Ubisoft Quartz, GameStop NFT (che ha citato 'regulatory uncertainty' chiudendo). Royal.io è morta sul muro Howey: qualsiasi feature di resale/royalty/profit-share trasforma il token in una security. Il framing della tesi — 'ogni acquisto minta un NFT nel wallet' — è esattamente il pattern che ha catturato solo speculatori e allontanato i consumatori normali. I sopravvissuti (Sound Premium, Book.io) hanno reso la chain infrastruttura invisibile.
  - *Mitigation*: Nascondere completamente la chain: embedded wallet + passkey (EIP-7702, 62M smart account attivi, paymaster per gas invisibile) con export-to-self-custody garantito come hard requirement. Token strettamente consumptive (proof-of-license, mai profit expectation). Resale differita a tempo indeterminato e comunque solo come termine contrattuale concesso dal rights holder (eccezione: trasferimenti UsedSoft-compliant per software EU). Il pitch è 'la tua ricevuta sopravvive allo store', mai 'NFT'.
- **Economia e liability dell'hosting distribuito del catalogo non tornano** — Storage warm decentralizzato costa $5-10k/PB/mese più egress ~$7/TB; il catalogo globale film+giochi in master di qualità è centinaia di petabyte, in gran parte contenuto che nessuno compra — nessun modello di revenue lo copre. 'I nodi ospitano collettivamente' scarica costi ed esposizione legale su volontari che, se anonimi, non possono nemmeno invocare i safe harbor §512 (serve designated agent e repeat-infringer policy). E i chokepoint non-tecnici restano: itch.io è stata piegata dai payment processor a luglio 2025 — la coercizione passa dai rail di pagamento, non dal protocollo.
  - *Mitigation*: Registry content-free by design: registra licenze, non ospita né vende contenuto (rischio copyright quasi zero). Contenuto solo in due tier accountable: artefatti autorizzati dai rights holder su storage pagato dalla membership, e tier di preservazione presso nodi istituzionali identificabili con piena compliance §512/DSA. Architettura a due livelli: infrastruttura operata accountable + peer thin di sola verifica che non fanno mai relay di contenuto. Mai contenuto non autorizzato in rete.
- **Il tailwind regolatorio è disclosure-shaped, non mandate-shaped — e la finestra è stretta** — Il fondatore spera nella spinta EU, ma la Commissione ha risposto a 1,29M firme SKG il 16 giugno 2026 con ZERO legislazione: solo un codice di condotta volontario entro fine 2026. AB 2426 e Maryland HB 208 impongono etichette oneste ('non puoi dire buy se è revocabile'), non portabilità né registri aperti. Nessun regolatore sta per costringere gli incumbent sulle rails di qualcuno. Contare sulla coercizione regolatoria come piano di adozione è una scommessa a bassa probabilità con timeline pluriennale, mentre la carve-out startup di CDSM art 17 (<3 anni/<€10M) scade nel frattempo.
  - *Mitigation*: Invertire la postura: vendere compliance invece di aspettare coercizione. Diventare la reference implementation tecnica del codice di condotta EU (deposito end-of-life, clausole di survivability nello schema di licenza), offrire agli store lo schema di disclosure AB 2426-ready — nota il design opportunity: un entitlement tecnicamente irrevocabile è ESENTE dagli obblighi di disclosure. Fare lobbying dentro il processo Digital Fairness Act (proposta Q3 2026) e con i consumer group ORA, finché la finestra 2026 è aperta.
- **'Finché il buyer tiene le sue chiavi' è una contraddizione UX al mass market** — Self-custody obbligatoria è un UX killer documentato, ma custody pura è ownership finta (di nuovo Robot Cache). A scala mainstream, la perdita di chiavi produrrebbe più librerie perse della revoca da piattaforma che il progetto vuole curare — un fallimento peggiore del problema. Lo stack 2026 mitiga (EIP-7702 live, passkey secp256r1, 62M smart account) ma l'infrastruttura sottostante è instabile: Lit ha ciclato tre generazioni di rete in sei mesi, TACo affronta un fork nel H2 2026.
  - *Mitigation*: Onboarding embedded/custodial di default con export-to-self-custody garantito e testato; ricevute firmate verificabili OFFLINE indipendentemente dalla chain (la verifica non deve richiedere né wallet né RPC); layer chiavi/threshold-decryption astratto dietro interfacce proprie (envelope DEK per-contenuto) per ruotare i provider senza ri-criptare il corpus.

### Salvageable core

Il nucleo che sopravvive a tutti i kill-factor è il registro di licenze aperto, neutrale e content-free: ricevute d'acquisto firmate crittograficamente, esportabili dall'utente, verificabili offline, replicate su nodi indipendenti, progettate per sopravvivere a qualunque store — inclusi i fondatori — e accoppiate a un diritto contrattuale garantito di re-download di un artefatto DRM-free con content hash e obblighi di mirror. Lanciato non come piattaforma universale ma come standard condiviso del verticale PC gaming DRM-free (GOG come anchor, itch.io secondo, publisher indie a costo marginale zero) e come reference implementation della compliance emergente (AB 2426, codice di condotta EU end-of-life, Digital Fairness Act). Sopravvive anche il pilastro preservazione, ma solo in forma separata e legalmente partizionata: federazione di istituzioni qualificate su base CDSM art 6 (modello BnF/EFGAMP), accesso tiered per giurisdizione, materiali contestuali aperti, nessun remote play pubblico. 'La tua ricevuta sopravvive allo store' è vero, difendibile, domandato e regolatoriamente allineato. 'Compra ovunque, possiedi tutto per sempre, archivio universale di tutto' è la parte fantasy.

### Sequencing advice

Primo: validare la willingness-to-pay prima di scrivere codice — la lezione Robot Cache ($532/anno) è che la domanda vocale non paga; servono esperimenti a impegno economico reale con publisher indie e utenti GOG/itch. Secondo (in parallelo, costo basso): pubblicare la spec aperta del registro content-free — record di entitlement portabili, verifica offline, capability flag per-giurisdizione, campi survivability/end-of-life e commercial-availability, modello ERC-5218-style ma chain-hidden — perché nessuno standard ha vinto nel 2026 e pubblicare per primi crea lo Schelling point. Terzo: chiudere GOG come anchor partner (mission-aligned, appena indipendente, già opera un proto-registry che non può credibilmente essere single-company dopo Robot Cache) e itch.io come secondo; onboarding publisher = flag di metadata, mai integration project. Quarto: cavalcare la finestra regolatoria con scadenze reali — processo code-of-conduct EU entro fine 2026, proposta Digital Fairness Act Q3 2026, arco narrativo Sony disc-cutoff gennaio 2028 — posizionandosi come implementazione tecnica di riferimento; considerare il wedge B2B/compliance prima del marketplace consumer (è l'unico posizionamento ben capitalizzato sopravvissuto nel 2025-2026). Quinto: solo dopo l'adozione del registro, il layer artefatti (re-download garantito, storage Filecoin/Storj tiered, delivery via CDN) e l'embedded wallet UX. Sesto: il pilastro preservazione parte come progetto istituzionale separato (partner EFGAMP-style, CDSM art 6, deposito volontario end-of-life) e non si mescola mai col marketplace. Differire indefinitamente: resale, integrazione incumbent, hosting di contenuto non autorizzato, self-custody obbligatoria, video e console. La ratio del sequencing: ogni fase de-rischia la successiva — WTP prima della spec, spec prima dei partner, partner prima del codice pesante, e il pilastro legalmente pericoloso (archivio) isolato dall'inizio così un suo fallimento non uccide il registro.

### Verdict

La tesi così com'è formulata muore su tre muri verificati: gli incumbent non adottano registri neutrali (UltraViolet aveva quasi tutti gli studios e 30M utenti ed è morto per il rifiuto di Apple/Amazon/Google; il loro 30% non è in vendita perché il vero asset è il lock-in), l'archivio universale è infringement di massa che né crittografia né decentralizzazione riparano (e il suo stesso marketing è inducement alla Grokster), e la domanda di ownership è vocale ma non monetaria ($532/anno allo store più ownership-forward mai esistito). 'Diventare così buoni da essere inevitabili' è letteralmente la strategia con cui sono morti tutti i predecessori degli ultimi vent'anni. Sopravvive però un nucleo reale, urgente e temporalmente favorito: lo standard aperto di ricevute portabili per l'ecosistema DRM-free PC, con GOG come ancora, la finestra regolatoria 2026 come leva e una federazione istituzionale di preservazione come pilastro separato — piccolo, noioso, legale e costruibile. Il fondatore deve scegliere tra la visione totale (che ha probabilità di sopravvivenza vicina a zero) e questa versione ridotta che può davvero esistere fra dieci anni.
